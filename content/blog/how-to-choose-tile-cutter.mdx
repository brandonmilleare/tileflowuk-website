Picking the wrong tile cutter is one of the most expensive mistakes you can make on a tiling job — not because the tool costs you money directly, but because bad cuts cost you time, tiles, and your reputation. I've been tiling professionally for over ten years, and I still see tradespeople hauling the wrong cutter to site every week.

This guide cuts through the noise. By the end, you'll know exactly which cutter belongs on each type of job — and which brands are actually worth spending money on.

## Manual vs Electric: How Do You Actually Decide?

The honest answer most tool guides won't give you: **manual cutters handle 85–90% of residential tiling work** faster, cleaner, and with less mess than an electric saw ([RUBI Tools](https://www.rubi.com/en/blog/tile-cutters/), 2024). Electric saws exist for situations manual cutters genuinely can't handle — not as an upgrade you need by default.

{/* [PERSONAL EXPERIENCE] */}
I carry both on every job. My manual cutter comes out first, every time. The wet saw stays in the van until I actually need it.

Here's my decision process:

**Reach for your manual cutter when:**
- You're cutting straight lines in ceramic, porcelain up to 10–12mm, or natural stone slabs
- You're on a domestic bathroom or kitchen job with standard-format tiles
- You need speed — a good manual cutter scores and snaps in under 10 seconds per tile
- Dust is a problem — manual cutters produce almost none

**Get the wet saw out when:**
- Tile thickness exceeds 12mm (thick porcelain, slate, travertine)
- You need L-cuts, mitres, or notches that a manual cutter physically can't make
- You're cutting mosaic sheets, small pieces, or irregular shapes
- The tile is extremely hard and the manual cutter is chipping the glaze

If you're doing a mix of both on one job — which happens constantly — you'll be switching between tools all day. That's normal. The goal is knowing which tool to reach for without thinking about it.

## What Size Cutter Do You Actually Need?

Cutter size is where most people get this wrong. The cutting length listed on the box refers to the maximum straight cut the cutter can make. You need a cutter rated for your **largest tile** — not your average tile.

Here's a practical breakdown:

| Cutter Size | Best For |
|-------------|----------|
| 37–60cm | Small format tiles, metro tiles, mosaics, kitchens |
| 70–75cm | Standard bathroom floor tiles, 600×600mm format |
| 90–100cm | Large format tiles, 800×800mm, 600×1200mm |
| 120–130cm | Slab format tiles, 1200×1200mm, feature walls |

{/* [UNIQUE INSIGHT] */}
One thing I've noticed over the years: professionals consistently undersize their cutters because they base the decision on the tile they're cutting most often, not the tile they're cutting occasionally. A 70cm cutter might handle 90% of your tiles fine, but that one diagonal cut on a 600×600mm tile on a mitred edge will defeat it. Size up — your back will thank you too, since longer arms mean less effort per cut.

**My go-to for most domestic jobs: 70cm.** It covers the vast majority of residential tile formats without being awkward to transport.

## Sigma or RUBI — Which Brand Is Worth Your Money?

There are three professional-grade manual tile cutter brands: Sigma, RUBI, and Montolit. Everything else is trade-counter filler. For UK tilers, Sigma and RUBI dominate, and the choice between them is genuinely close.

**Sigma** is made in Italy and has been setting the standard for manual tile cutters for decades. The pull-action handle is distinctive — you score by pulling toward you rather than pushing away — and it gives exceptional control on longer cuts. The carbide scoring wheel runs at a high angle that penetrates dense porcelain with less downward force, which means less chipping on rectified edges.

**RUBI** is Spanish-made and slightly more affordable. The range is wide, the quality is consistent, and for softer materials — ceramic, glazed wall tiles — they cut cleanly and quickly. Where RUBI falls slightly behind Sigma is on thick, large-format porcelain: Sigma's spring-loaded breaking foot handles these better.

> According to professional tilers across UK forums and job sites, Sigma wins on porcelain and hard stone while RUBI holds its own on ceramic and glazed tile — the brand you choose often comes down to what material you tile most ([Tilers Forums](https://www.tilersforums.com/threads/sigma-or-rubi.77108/), 2023).

## What About Electric Tile Saws — Do You Need One?

If you're a professional tiler working full-time, yes. Not as your primary tool, but as part your setup.

Electric wet saws — sometimes called tile wet saws or diamond blade saws — use a water-cooled diamond disc to cut through any tile material regardless of thickness, hardness, or angle. They're slower than manual cutters for straight cuts, messier, and louder, but they handle cuts that are simply impossible any other way.

For L-cuts around plug sockets, mitred edges on feature walls, notches for pipe runs, and cutting through thick natural stone — a wet saw is the only option.

**What makes a good wet saw?** Look for:
- Stable sliding table (wobble here causes bad cuts)
- At least 900mm of rip capacity for large format tiles
- Continuous water feed to the blade (not just a spray)
- A powerful enough motor that it doesn't bog down in hard porcelain

I'd rather have a mid-range wet saw from a brand like DEWALT or Battipav than a cheap import — the stability of the table matters more than raw power.

## The Cutter Checklist Before You Buy

Before spending money on a new tile cutter, run through these:

1. **What's the largest tile I'll regularly cut?** Add 10cm to that and buy a cutter rated to that length.
2. **What materials am I cutting?** Porcelain → Sigma. Ceramic mix → RUBI is fine.
3. **Do I need complex cuts?** If yes, budget for a wet saw too — a manual cutter won't do it.
4. **How often will I use it?** Daily professional use → buy proper. Occasional DIY → mid-range is fine.
5. **Is the scoring wheel replaceable?** On any cutter worth buying, yes. Check before you commit.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can a manual tile cutter cut porcelain?

Yes — most quality manual cutters handle porcelain up to 10–12mm thick cleanly. Thicker than that, or very hard full-body porcelain, and you'll get chipping. Sigma's high-angle carbide wheel performs better on dense porcelain than most other brands ([RUBI Tools](https://www.rubi.com/en/blog/best-tile-cutter/), 2024).

### How long does a tile cutter scoring wheel last?

On a quality cutter like Sigma or RUBI, a scoring wheel used daily by a professional tiler should last 1–3 years before needing replacement. Cheaper cutters may need new wheels every few months. Replacement wheels cost £15–£30 and take minutes to swap.

### Is it worth buying a cheap tile cutter?

For DIY jobs — possibly. For professional work — no. A cheap cutter gives inconsistent scoring pressure, which causes chipping and breakage. The cost of broken tiles on one job will exceed what you'd have saved buying a quality cutter.

### What's the difference between a tile cutter and a tile saw?

A tile cutter (manual) scores and snaps tile using a carbide wheel and a breaking foot — no water, no electricity, minimal dust. A tile saw (wet saw) uses a water-cooled diamond blade to cut through tile like a circular saw. Cutters are faster for straight cuts; saws handle complex cuts and hard materials.

### Can I use a manual tile cutter on wall tiles?

Absolutely — wall tiles are typically thinner and softer than floor tiles, making them the easiest material for a manual cutter. Even a mid-range cutter handles ceramic wall tiles cleanly.

## The Bottom Line

Don't overcomplicate the decision. For most residential tiling work in the UK, a quality manual cutter in the right size handles everything you'll encounter day to day. Sigma for porcelain-heavy work, RUBI if budget matters. Add a wet saw for the jobs that genuinely need one.

Buy once, buy right — a good cutter will outlast five cheap ones.
