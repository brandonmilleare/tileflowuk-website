Measure the area in square metres, add 10% for wastage, then divide by the size of one tile. That's the whole method — and it's the same sum I do on every quote, whether it's a downstairs loo or a 40 m² kitchen-diner.

If you'd rather skip the maths, **[use our tile calculator](/tile-calculator)** — put your measurements in and it does the lot, wastage included.

## Key takeaways

- **Area to tile (m²) ÷ area of one tile (m²) = tiles needed.** Add wastage before you divide.
- **10% wastage** for straight or brick-bond, **15%** for herringbone, diagonal, or large-format tiles.
- **Always round up to whole boxes** — and keep one spare box sealed for repairs.
- **Measure the room in metres**, the tile in millimetres (it's on the box).
- For an awkward or L-shaped room, **split it into rectangles** and add them up.

## Step 1: measure the area

Measure the length and width of each surface you're tiling and multiply them. A bathroom floor that's 4m long and 3m wide is **12 m²** (4 × 3).

Tiling more than one surface — say all four walls of a shower? Measure each one, work out its area, and add them together. Don't try to be clever with one big sum; do each wall on its own and total them at the end. Fewer mistakes that way.

If the room's an L-shape or has a chimney breast, split it into rectangles in your head, measure each rectangle separately, and add them. That's exactly what the **[calculator's "add another area" button](/tile-calculator)** is for.

## Step 2: take off the big gaps

You don't tile over a doorway, a window, or a fitted vanity unit. Measure those, work out their area, and subtract them from your total.

Be sensible about it, mind. Knocking off a single light switch isn't worth the bother — that's what wastage is for. But a 0.8 × 2.1m door opening (1.68 m²) or a big picture window is worth taking off, especially on a wall.

## Step 3: add your wastage

Wastage is the spare you order on top, because no job uses 100% of what you buy. Cuts, the odd breakage, a tile that chips on the corner, and a box kept back for future repairs — it all comes out of that margin.

Here's what I add, by lay pattern:

| Lay pattern | Wastage |
|---|---|
| Straight | 10% |
| Brick bond | 10% |
| Herringbone | 15% |
| Diagonal | 15% |
| Large-format (600mm+) in a small room | 15% |

Herringbone and diagonal lays eat more tile because nearly every edge tile gets cut at an angle, and the offcut often can't be used anywhere else. **[Large-format tiles](/blog/large-format-tile-installation-guide)** are similar — fewer tiles overall, but every cut wastes a bigger piece, so a small room can need 15% rather than 10%.

So our 12 m² floor on a straight lay becomes **12 × 1.10 = 13.2 m²** to order.

## Step 4: divide by the tile size

Now divide your area-to-order by the area of a single tile.

A 600 × 600mm tile is 0.6m × 0.6m = **0.36 m²**. So:

**13.2 ÷ 0.36 = 36.7 → 37 tiles.**

Always round up — you can't order 0.7 of a tile, and coming up short mid-job is the one thing you don't want.

### Tiles per m², for the common sizes

| Tile size | Tiles per m² |
|---|---|
| 600 × 600mm | ~2.8 |
| 300 × 600mm | ~5.6 |
| 300 × 300mm | ~11.1 |
| 200 × 250mm | ~20 |

These are bare figures with no wastage — handy for a rough gut-check, but run the real numbers (with wastage) before you buy.

## Step 5: round up to whole boxes

Tiles are sold by the box, not the single tile, and box quantities vary with tile size — anything from 3 large-format tiles to a dozen small ones. Check the box quantity on the product, work out how many boxes covers your tile count, and **round up to the whole box**.

That rounding often nudges you a bit past your 10%, which is fine. The one rule I'd never break: **keep a full box back, sealed, after the job.** Tile colours are mixed in batches, and a replacement box bought eighteen months later almost never matches. A spare box in the loft is the cheapest insurance there is.
