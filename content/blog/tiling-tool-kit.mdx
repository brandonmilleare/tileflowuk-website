If you're buying tools to tile, spend on the cutter, the level, and a proper trowel — and don't waste money on gadgets that promise to replace skill. That's the short version. The longer version is this: here's the actual kit I carry, ranked honestly by what earns its keep, so you put your money where it counts.

This is the tools hub — it pulls together every tool guide and review on the site. Use it as the map, then click into the deep-dive on whatever you're buying.

> **Key Takeaways**
> - **Spend first on the cutter.** A good one saves tiles; a cheap one wastes them.
> - **A manual cutter + an angle grinder** covers most domestic jobs.
> - **A laser level** is the upgrade that pays back fastest on bigger jobs.
> - **Match the trowel notch to the tile** — too small and the tile won't bond.
> - **Skip the gimmicks.** No gadget replaces a steady hand and a set-out.

## Buy now: the core kit

These are the tools you can't tile without. Buy these well.

### Tile cutter

The single most important tool. A manual tile cutter scores and snaps straight cuts cleanly and fast. Buy a good one — a weak cutter cracks tiles, and especially struggles with porcelain. For choosing, see **[how to choose a tile cutter](/blog/how-to-choose-tile-cutter)**, the head-to-head in **[Sigma vs RUBI](/blog/sigma-vs-rubi-tile-cutters)**, and the decision on whether you also need a wet saw in **[tile cutter vs wet saw](/blog/tile-cutter-vs-wet-saw)**.

### Angle grinder

The cheap workhorse that fills the gap the cutter can't reach — curved cuts, L-shapes around sockets and pipes, and notches. Fit a diamond blade. A manual cutter and a grinder between them handle most domestic tiling.

### Notched trowel

Spreads adhesive in even ridges so the tile beds properly. The notch size has to match the tile — too small and the tile won't get full contact and bond, too big and you waste adhesive. Match it to your tile, and step up the notch for large format. **[The tile adhesive buying guide](/guides/tile-adhesive-buying-guide)** covers trowel-to-tile sizing.

### Spirit level

Non-negotiable for setting out level and plumb. A decent 1.2m level minimum for walls and floors.

### Spacers and levelling clips

Spacers keep your joints consistent; levelling clips keep adjacent tile edges flush and stop lippage on bigger tiles. Which you need depends on the tile — **[tile spacers buying guide](/guides/tile-spacers-buying-guide)** and **[tile levelling clips explained](/guides/tile-levelling-clips-explained)** cover both.

### Mixing kit, grout float, sponge, silicone gun

A bucket and a slow-speed paddle mixer for adhesive and grout (don't mix by hand on anything but the smallest job), a grout float and a proper grout sponge for finishing, and a silicone gun for the movement joints. None of it glamorous, all of it needed.

## Buy later: the upgrades that earn their keep

You can start without these, but they make you quicker and more accurate as the jobs get bigger.

### Laser level

The upgrade that pays back fastest. A laser sets your datum and checks plumb in seconds, and it transforms floor and large-format work. A spirit level gets you going; a laser makes you quick. See **[laser levels for tiling](/blog/laser-levels-for-tiling)**.

### Wet saw

A water-cooled, diamond-blade saw for big porcelain, thick tiles, and lots of angled or L-shaped cuts. Most domestic jobs don't need one — but when the tile or the pattern demands it, nothing else will do. The picks are in **[best wet saw for tiling](/best-of/best-wet-saw-for-tiling)**, and the buy-or-not decision is in **[tile cutter vs wet saw](/blog/tile-cutter-vs-wet-saw)**.

### Knee pads, suction lifters, and the comfort kit

Decent knee pads (your knees will thank you in ten years), suction lifters for handling large-format tiles safely, and the bits that make a long day less brutal. Buy them when the work warrants them.

## Never bothered: skip these

- **Bargain-bin own-brand cutters.** A cutter that cracks tiles costs more in wasted tiles than a good one costs to buy.
- **Single-use gadgets** that promise to do one niche thing — most end up in a drawer.
- **Anything that promises to replace skill.** Set-out, a steady hand, and patience aren't sold in a box.
