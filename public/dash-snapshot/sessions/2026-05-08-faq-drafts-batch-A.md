### how-to-choose-tile-cutter

```yaml
faqs:
  - q: "Can a manual tile cutter cut porcelain?"
    a: "A quality manual cutter handles porcelain up to about 10–12mm thick cleanly. Beyond that, or with very hard full-body porcelain, you'll start getting chipped breaks. Sigma's high-angle carbide wheel copes with dense porcelain better than most other brands, which is why it's my daily driver for porcelain-heavy jobs."
  - q: "What size tile cutter do I actually need?"
    a: "Size to your largest tile, not your average one. A 70cm cutter covers most UK domestic bathrooms and kitchens. Step up to 95cm for 800×800mm or 600×1200mm large format, and 120cm+ for slabs. Undersize a cutter and that one diagonal cut will defeat it every time."
  - q: "Sigma or RUBI — which is the better tile cutter?"
    a: "Sigma's heavier frame and high-angle carbide wheel give cleaner breaks on dense porcelain over 10mm. RUBI is well-built and cuts ceramic and standard porcelain reliably for less money. For full-time porcelain work, Sigma. For mixed ceramic and budget-conscious jobs, RUBI Slim G2 is solid value."
  - q: "How long does a tile cutter scoring wheel last?"
    a: "On a Sigma or RUBI used daily by a professional, a scoring wheel lasts 1–3 years before it needs replacing. Cheaper cutters often need new wheels every few months. Replacements cost £15–£30 and swap in minutes — check your wheel is replaceable before you buy any cutter."
  - q: "Do I need a wet saw as well as a manual cutter?"
    a: "If you tile full-time, yes — but as a second tool, not your main one. Manual cutters handle 85–90% of residential straight cuts faster and cleaner. Reach for the wet saw only for L-cuts around sockets, mitred edges, pipe notches, or tile thicker than 12mm where the manual will chip."
  - q: "Is it worth buying a cheap tile cutter?"
    a: "For occasional DIY, possibly. For trade work, no. Cheap cutters give inconsistent scoring pressure, which causes chipping and broken tiles. The cost of wasted tiles on a single bathroom job will wipe out anything you saved on the cutter. Buy once, buy right — a Sigma 4BU outlasts five budget cutters."
  - q: "What's the difference between a tile cutter and a tile saw?"
    a: "A manual tile cutter scores the tile with a carbide wheel and snaps it on a breaking foot — no water, no power, almost no dust. A wet saw uses a water-cooled diamond blade to cut through tile like a circular saw. Cutters are faster for straight cuts; saws handle hard materials and complex shapes."
```

### how-to-tile-a-bathroom-floor

```yaml
faqs:
  - q: "How long does it take to tile a bathroom floor?"
    a: "A typical UK bathroom of 4–6m² takes a competent DIYer a full day: half a day for prep and layout, half a day laying tiles. Add another half day for grouting once the adhesive has cured for 24 hours. Rushing the prep is what stretches the job into a weekend disaster."
  - q: "Should I tile from the centre or from a wall?"
    a: "Always from the centre. Walls are rarely square and starting from one will splay your tiles out as you go. Snap a chalk line cross at the room centre, dry-lay along both axes, and shift the cross by half a tile if any edge cut comes out as a sliver. Half-tile cuts look intentional; 5mm slivers look like a mistake."
  - q: "Can I tile directly onto a timber bathroom floor?"
    a: "Not without protection. Timber flexes and standard ceramic will crack — sometimes within months. Use a decoupling mat (DITRA-style) which absorbs movement, or screw down 6mm or 12mm Hardibacker at 200mm centres. Either way, finish with an S1 flexible adhesive on top."
  - q: "What adhesive should I use for a bathroom floor?"
    a: "An S1 class flexible adhesive, every time. Rigid adhesives crack with any building movement, and floors with underfloor heating need that flex even more. Mix with a slow-speed drill and paddle, let it slake for five minutes, then mix again — that's what activates the polymers."
  - q: "Do I need waterproofing under bathroom floor tiles?"
    a: "For a standard bathroom with a sealed bath or shower tray, full tanking under the floor isn't strictly required. For a wet room or any walk-in shower with a linear drain, full tanking is essential — the tiles and grout aren't the waterproof layer, the membrane underneath is."
  - q: "How big should the grout joints be on a bathroom floor?"
    a: "3mm for standard bathroom floor tiles is the working standard, using sanded floor grout. For rectified large format tiles, drop to 1.5–2mm with an unsanded grout. And the perimeter gap of 5–8mm round the room gets filled with matching silicone, never grout — silicone flexes, grout cracks."
  - q: "Can I tile over existing bathroom tiles?"
    a: "Technically yes, but only if the old tiles are fully bonded with no hollow spots, the subfloor can take the extra weight, and the new height won't foul door thresholds. Prime with an SBR bonding agent first. In most cases I'd lift the old tiles — you find out what the substrate is really doing."
```

### large-format-tile-installation-guide

```yaml
faqs:
  - q: "Do I need special tools for large format tiles?"
    a: "A tile cutter rated for 95cm or longer (the Sigma 4DN handles up to 95cm), suction cups for handling the tiles safely, and lippage control clips and wedges are all required. Trying to lay 600×1200mm tiles with standard kit is the main reason DIY large format jobs fail at the lippage stage."
  - q: "How flat does the substrate need to be for large format tiles?"
    a: "Within 3mm deviation across any 2-metre span — tighter than the 5mm tolerance for standard tiles. Anything over 3mm shows as lippage between tiles, where one edge sits visibly higher than the next. Check with a 2m straightedge in all directions before you mix any adhesive."
  - q: "Can I lay 600×1200mm tiles onto a timber floor?"
    a: "Possible but high risk. Timber flex and large format don't mix well. If you can't avoid it, use a full decoupling mat like DITRA Drain plus an S2 super-flexible adhesive — and accept you're at the limits of what the system handles. On any new bathroom build I'd specify a backer board or screed instead."
  - q: "What grout joint should I use for large format tiles?"
    a: "1.5mm to 2mm for rectified large format porcelain — the precise edges allow tight joints. Non-rectified large format may need 3mm to absorb slight size variations between tiles. Use unsanded or fine-grain grout at this width, or epoxy grout for wet rooms where stain resistance matters."
  - q: "Why do large format tiles crack after installation?"
    a: "Three usual causes: voids in the adhesive bed (hollow spots that flex under load), a substrate too soft or out of tolerance, or a rigid adhesive with no S1/S2 flexibility rating. Any one of these creates a stress point that a 600×1200mm tile can't absorb the way a smaller tile would."
  - q: "Is back-buttering large format tiles really necessary?"
    a: "Mandatory, not optional. A 600×1200mm tile cannot bridge over a small hollow in the bed the way a mosaic can — it will rock or crack. A thin skim coat on the back of every tile, on top of a notched bed on the floor, is what gets you to the 100% coverage large format needs."
  - q: "What's the right adhesive class for large format porcelain?"
    a: "Minimum EN 12004 class C2 S1 — flexible, high-adhesion. For commercial floors, underfloor heating, or anything with movement risk, step up to C2 S2 super-flexible. Don't use basic wall-and-floor adhesive on large format floor tiles; it doesn't have the flex to handle thermal movement of a big slab."
```

### laser-levels-for-tiling

```yaml
faqs:
  - q: "Do I need a laser level for tiling or is a spirit level enough?"
    a: "A spirit level works but is significantly slower — you check every reference point one at a time. A 3×360° laser projects continuous reference lines across the entire room at once, which speeds up layout and stops cumulative error building up across a large floor. Once you've used one, you don't go back."
  - q: "Self-levelling or manual laser level — which is right for tiling?"
    a: "Self-levelling, every time. Most subfloors have minor variation, and a manual laser means constant rechecking and recalibrating. A self-levelling model compensates automatically within ±4° or ±5° — you set it down, it levels itself, you start tiling. The price gap is small and the time saved is huge."
  - q: "How accurate does a laser level need to be for tiling work?"
    a: "For domestic tiling, ±2mm/10m is workable. For trade work or large commercial floors where errors compound over distance, look for ±1mm/10m. The DEWALT DCE089D1G and Huepar Pro both hit that pro standard. Anything less accurate than ±2mm/10m isn't worth using on a tile floor."
  - q: "Green or red laser beam for tiling?"
    a: "Green, unless budget is tight. Green laser beams are roughly four times more visible to the human eye than red in lit conditions. On a modern open-plan kitchen or bathroom with good natural light, a red beam can be hard to see at distance. The slight extra cost pays for itself in fewer squinting moments."
  - q: "What's the difference between a 3×360° and a standard cross-line laser?"
    a: "A standard cross-line laser projects one horizontal and one vertical line — useful but limited. A 3×360° laser projects three full 360-degree planes (one horizontal, two vertical at 90°) that wrap right around the room. For tiling, that gives you square in both directions and a continuous floor datum at the same time."
  - q: "Can I use a laser level for wall tiling as well as floors?"
    a: "Absolutely — the two vertical planes are exactly what wall tiling needs. Project a plumb vertical line floor to ceiling and you can set your first row dead upright regardless of how out-of-square the room is. The dual vertical planes also let you align tiles around a corner without re-measuring."
  - q: "How often do laser levels need calibrating?"
    a: "Check yours every few months, or after any rough handling on site. Quick test: project a horizontal line, mark it on a wall, rotate the laser 180°, see if the line still matches. If it's out by more than 2mm at the same distance, the laser needs recalibrating or replacing — most manufacturers offer service."
```
