### how-to-choose-tile-grout

```yaml
faqs:
  - q: "What grout should I use for bathroom floor tiles?"
    a: "Sanded floor grout for joints 3mm and over — ideally one with anti-mould or biocide protection. For large format rectified tiles with 1.5mm joints, switch to unsanded or epoxy grout, because sanded grout won't compress into a 1.5mm gap properly. Mapei Ultracolor Plus and BAL Micromax 2 are reliable options."
  - q: "Is epoxy grout worth the extra cost?"
    a: "For wet rooms and kitchen splashbacks above the hob, epoxy is worth it. It costs 3 to 4 times more than cement grout, but the stain resistance and longevity pay back over years. For a dry bathroom wall with standard tiles, cement grout sealed properly will do the job."
  - q: "Can I grout over old grout to refresh it?"
    a: "No. New grout applied over old grout won't bond properly and will fail within months. Rake the old grout out first with a grout rake (around £10 to £15 from any tile shop), brush the joints clean, then apply fresh grout. Anything else is a bodge."
  - q: "What's the difference between sanded and unsanded grout?"
    a: "Unsanded grout is fine-grain and smooth, used for joints up to 3mm — typical wall tiles and narrow-jointed floors. Sanded grout contains fine sand for compressive strength and is used for joints 3mm and above on floors. Sanded particles will scratch polished tiles, so always use unsanded on those."
  - q: "Why does white grout turn yellow over time?"
    a: "Cleaning products containing bleach are the main cause — they discolour white cement grout over months. Hard water deposits and soap residue add to it. Use pH-neutral tile cleaners only on white grout, seal it after installation, and clean with a mild solution to keep it looking right."
  - q: "Do I need to seal cement grout?"
    a: "Yes for wet rooms, kitchen splashbacks, and floor tiles. Cement grout is porous and will absorb moisture, grease, and cleaning products. Apply a penetrating sealer like Mapei Grout Sealer or BAL Microguard after the grout has fully cured — minimum 72 hours. Epoxy grout doesn't need sealing."
  - q: "What grout joint width should I use?"
    a: "1.5mm for rectified large format, 2 to 3mm for standard wall and floor tiles, 4 to 6mm for rustic or non-rectified tiles. The tile manufacturer usually sets the recommended width. Rectified tiles allow narrow joints; non-rectified tiles need wider joints to absorb size variation between pieces."
```

### subfloor-prep-before-tiling

```yaml
faqs:
  - q: "Can I tile directly onto a chipboard or OSB floor?"
    a: "No. Chipboard and OSB both expand significantly when wet and move with humidity. Tiles laid directly onto either will fail, sometimes within weeks. Either fit a decoupling mat (DITRA or similar) over the timber with S1 flexible adhesive, or screw down 6mm or 12mm cement backer board first."
  - q: "Do I need to prime concrete before tiling?"
    a: "Yes, always. Primer reduces the porosity of the concrete so it doesn't suck moisture out of the adhesive too quickly, and it improves bond strength. Use Bal Prime AP, Mapei Primer G, or Ardex P51, diluted 1:3 with water, applied with a brush or roller and left until tacky."
  - q: "How flat does my subfloor need to be for tiling?"
    a: "For standard tiles up to 400mm: maximum 5mm deviation over 2 metres. For large format tiles 600mm and over: maximum 3mm over 2 metres. Use a 1.2m spirit level in multiple directions to check. Anything worse needs a self-levelling compound — never try to fix it with thicker adhesive beds."
  - q: "Can I tile over existing tiles?"
    a: "Sometimes. Tap every existing tile with a coin — a hollow sound means it's loose and must come up. Check the combined height (8 to 12mm extra) won't foul doorways or skirtings. Apply a bonding primer like BAL Bond SBR to the glazed surface, then tile with S1 flexible adhesive."
  - q: "What's the difference between a decoupling mat and backer board?"
    a: "A decoupling mat (DITRA) is a polyethylene mat bonded to timber that absorbs movement before it reaches the tile — best on older, springier suspended floors. Backer board (Jackoboard, Hardiebacker) is a rigid cement board screwed to the timber — best on stiff, well-fixed floors with minimal movement."
  - q: "How long should I wait after using levelling compound before tiling?"
    a: "Most compounds are ready for tiling in 3 to 24 hours, depending on the product and depth poured. Check the manufacturer's data sheet and don't rush it. Tiling over a compound that hasn't fully cured is the third most common cause of failed tiling jobs, after skipping primer and using the wrong adhesive on timber."
  - q: "Do I need a waterproof membrane in a wet room?"
    a: "Yes. Full tanking is essential for wet rooms and walk-in showers. Apply a waterproof membrane like BAL Waterproof Plus, Mapei Mapelastic, or Schlüter DITRA-DRAIN over the entire floor and up the walls to at least 300mm — ideally full height in the shower zone. Apply in two coats, paying attention to corners."
```

For tiles longer than 95cm, or anything thicker than 12mm, or any L-shaped cut, you need a wet saw — manual cutters only do straight breaks."
```yaml
  - q: "What is lippage and why do I need clips for it?"
    a: "Lippage is when one tile sits fractionally higher than the next. On large format floors, even 1mm of lippage catches the light and is visible from across the room — over 2mm looks like building site finish. Lippage clips and wedges pull adjacent tiles to the same level while the adhesive sets, then break off cleanly."
  - q: "Do I need suction cups for large format tiles?"
    a: "Yes. A 600x1200mm 10mm porcelain tile is too heavy and too smooth to grip safely by hand, and dropping one costs £60 to £150. Suction cups give a secure grip and let you lower the tile vertically onto the adhesive bed — never slide a large format tile across the bed, you'll wreck the notches."
  - q: "What's the flatness tolerance for large format tile installation?"
    a: "3mm over 2 metres. That's tight, which is why a self-levelling laser is non-negotiable on large format work. Set the laser in the centre of the room, use the horizontal plane as your datum, and check every tile against it. Without that, you're working by feel and lippage will creep in."
  - q: "Why do large format tiles fail more than standard tiles?"
    a: "Three reasons: the substrate isn't flat enough (tile bridges hollows and cracks under foot loading), incomplete adhesive coverage (back-buttering is essential, not optional), and lippage from no clip system. If you do large format regularly, the 4DN pays for itself in avoided breakages."
```
